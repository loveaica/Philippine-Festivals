Here are the background information
about Philippine Festivals

The origin of most early festivals, locally known as "fiestas", are rooted in Christianity, dating back to the Spanish colonial period when the many communities (such as barrios and towns) of the predominantly Catholic Philippines almost always had a patron saint assigned to each of them. Originally encouraged by the Spanish to coincide with Christian holy days, early patronal festivals became vital instruments in spreading Christianity throughout the country. Festivals in the Philippines can be religious, cultural, or both. Several of these are held to honor the local Roman Catholic patron saint, to commemorate local history and culture, to promote the community's products, or to celebrate a bountiful harvest.


The Philippines is an extremely culturally rich country. The Philippines celebrates a variety of its own festival that is celebrated in accordance with their culture, beliefs, and economic status.


Mabuhay means to “welcome” in Filipino. They use this as a greeting for guests, along with a wide smile on their faces. In the Philippines, festivals are classified into two types: Religious and Nonreligious. Religious Festivals honor specific religious icons and commemorate significant occurrences and concepts in a religion’s history and theology. Nonreligious Festivals are celebrated to give gratitude for a bountiful harvest and the success of the people's industries.



What is Religious Festival?

A religious festival is a time of special importance marked by adherents to that religion. Religious festivals are commonly celebrated on recurring cycles in a calendar year or lunar calendar. The science of religious rites and festivals is known as heortology. Religious, devout, pious indicate a spirit of reverence toward God. religious is a general word, applying to whatever pertains to faith or worship: a religious ceremony. Devout indicates a fervent spirit, usually genuine and often independent of outward observances: a deeply devout though unorthodox church member.

  1. Sinulog Festival



    The Sinulog Festival is a religious festival that is one of the Philippines’ largest and most popular. Cebu City honors Santo Niño (Child Jesus) on the 3rd Sunday of January. The Sinulog Festival has a grand parade, street parties, and religious processions. Dancers dressed in brightly colored costumes perform to the rhythm of drums and trumpets, resulting in a spectacular show. The festival also entails a tidal parade, where the image of the Santo Niño is carried by boat along the Mactan Channel. Novena masses, street parties, and live concerts are held before and after the main parade, adding to the festive atmosphere.

    This festival is considered to be the largest in the country, attracting between 1 million and 1.5 million people each year. It's celebrated in honor of Santo Niño, or the Holy Child Jesus and is known for its vibrant street parties and grand parade. The festival's name, Sinulog, comes from the Cebuano word "sulog", which means "water current".The dance associated with the festival consists of two steps forward and one step backward, done to the sound of drums, which is said to resemble the movement of a river's current.

    The Sinulog Festival has a rich history dating back to the arrival of the Spanish in the Philippines. According to historical accounts, the original image of Santo Niño was a baptismal gift from Ferdinand Magellan to Queen Juana of Cebu in 1521. This marked the beginning of Christianity in the Philippines. The festival today commemorates the Filipino people's acceptance of Christianity. The festival includes various activities such as a grand parade with vibrant costumes and festive music, a grand firework display, and sampling of local food. The phrase "Viva Pit Senyor!" is commonly heard during the festival, which translates to "Hail Lord, listen to our prayers!" Despite the ongoing pandemic, the Sinulog Festival continues to be celebrated with adjustments for safety protocols, including virtual mass.



    2. Sinulog Dance Steps

    The Sinulog dance steps are also believed to originate from Baladhay, Rajah Humabon's adviser.
    When Baladhay got sick, Humabon ordered his tribe to bring Baladhay into a room where the Santo Niño was enthroned. After a few days, Baladhay was heard shouting and was found dancing by the native tribe. Baladhay was asked as to why was he was shouting and dancing. He pointed at the image of the holy Jesus and explained that baby Jesus was tickling him.

    In turn, Baladhay danced with the little child and explained that he was dancing the movements of the river. To this day, the two-steps forward, the one-step backward movement is still used by Santo Niño devotees who believe that it was the Santo Niño's choice to have Baladhay dance.

  2. Pahiyas Festival



    The Pahiyas Festival, held in Lucban, Quezon, Philippines, is a vibrant celebration of faith and harvest. To honor San Isidro Labrador, the patron saint of farmers, an annual celebration is held on May 15. The elaborate and vibrant garlands which cover the town’s houses at the festival are well-known.

    The houses are covered with vibrant displays made from rice wafers, or “kiping,” fruits, vegetables, and other agricultural products during the Pahiyas Festival. These decorations serve as an act of thanking the harvest and featuring its abundance. The lively and vibrant Pahiyas Festival displays Lucban, Quezon’s creative spirit, agricultural legacy, and diverse culture. Residents as well as tourists are drawn to it as they want to take in the vibrant and joyous mood of this special occasion.

    Pahiyas came from two terms in the Philippines: ‘hiyas,’ which is a rare stone, and ‘payas,’ which means to decorate anything for an offering. The Pahiyas celebration translates to “presenting the most valuable pearls of the abundant crop”. The Pahiyas Festival began as a gift-giving rite by the people of Lucban to the Franciscan missionaries who brought Catholicism to Quezon in 1583. The town’s first church administrator, Juan de Placencia continued the practice of donating the crop to the Spanish friars. He did this as a sign of thanks for a fruitful harvest. The next parish priest, Diego de Oropesa, continued the rite until it became a Lucban regular.

  3. Ati-Atihan Festival



    Ati-Atihan Festival is an annual festival celebrated in Kalibo, Aklan on the third week of January. It marks a celebration of faith honoring the Santo Niño (Holy Child). As well as Atis, the local indigenous people who were believed to be the first inhabitants of Panay Island. The festival includes vibrant parades of locals wearing colorful costumes and masks and dancing to the rhythm of drums. Ati-Atihan is also known for its lively street parties, with several stages featuring live music and performances all throughout the day. There are other activities like Mass, processions, cultural shows, and beauty pageants that take place during the festival as well.

    The festival consists of religious processions and street-parades, showcasing themed floats, dancing groups wearing colorful costumes, marching bands, and people sporting face and body paints. The street parade is known as Sadsad, which is also what the locals call their way of dancing where the foot is momentarily dragged along the ground in tune to the beat played by the marching bands.

    Being believed to be the oldest festival in the Philippines and having inspired other popular Philippine festivals such as Dinagyang of Iloilo and Sinulog of Cebu, it is thus known as the "Mother of All Philippine Festivals."

    What is the history of Ati-Atihan?

    Ati-Atihan originated in the 13th century as a pagan festival. It began as a celebration of peace between the Aetas (indigenous people) of Panay Island and the Malaysia from Borneo who arrived at the island seeking a new place to live.


    There are two different theories about how the festival started.

    The first theory: To celebrate the peaceful coexistence of their people and the Aetas, the Malays colored their faces with soot to resemble and honor the indigenous population of the island.

    The second theory: After experiencing a bad harvest, the Malays went to the Aetas for help, and they shared their food and resources. According to this theory, the festival is a celebration of the generosity of the Aetas.

    Whichever theory is true, it’s clear that the festival began as a celebration of the kindness and generosity of the people of Panay Island.

  4. Black Nazarene Festival



    The Black Nazarene is an annual religious festival held in Quiapo, Manila every January 9th. It celebrates the miraculous survival of a black statue of Jesus Christ from a shipwreck during its voyage from Mexico to the Philippines. The event features a procession of devotees carrying the statue through the streets of Quiapo, amidst a flurry of chants and prayers.

    The Black Nazarene Festival is one of the most important religious events in the Philippines. Attracting devotees from all over the country and beyond.Part of the ritual includes participants rubbing pieces of cloth on the statue to absorb its divine powers.

    The procession also features colorful floats, marching bands, and other performances throughout the day. This unique event celebrates faith, tradition, and culture with an unforgettable display of devotion. Despite the heat and exhaustion that come with the almost day-long procession, many Filipino Catholics still choose to walk barefoot and wipe towels to the image, believing that the statue has miraculous attributes.

    It can heal sicknesses—even terminal ones, many followers say. Others believe it can grant petitions, help those in need and forgive sins. Quiapo Church officials estimated that around five million individuals will participate in the annual traslacion on Wednesday. This year’s event carries the theme “Hinirang at Pinili Upang Maging Lingkod Niya.”

  6. Moriones Festival



    The Moriones Festival is held during Holy Week (usually in April) on the island of Marinduque. This festival reenacts the story of Longinus,a Roman soldier who converted to Christianity after witnessing Jesus’ crucifixion.

    Men and women dress up in intricate costumes and masks resembling Roman soldiers and parade through town, while others participate in passion plays depicting biblical scenes.

    The Moriones Festival is one of the most anticipated events during Holy Week. Witnessing the festival is also a great opportunity to explore the island of Marinduque, especially its most popular attraction — Maniwaya Island.

    Every year during Holy Week, the Moriones Festival is a lenten and religious celebration on the Philippine island of Marinduque. According to the locals, the "Moriones" are men and women dressed like biblical Imperial Roman soldiers, complete with masks and costumes. Other street festivals in the Philippines that incorporate cultural customs have been spawned by the Moriones tradition.

    The term "Moriones" describes the group of disguised and masked penitents who spend seven days circling the town in quest of Longinus. From Holy Monday until Easter Sunday, morons prowl the town's streets, frightening children and attracting attention with their antics and surprises. At this folk-religious celebration, the life of Saint Longinus, a blind Roman centurion, is enacted. The celebration is distinguished by vibrantly colored tunics, painted masks and helmets, and elaborate Roman costumes. On the island of Marinduque, the municipalities of Boac, Gasan, Santa Cruz, Buenavista, and Mogpog combine to form one enormous stage. The observances are a component of Marinduque's Lenten festivities.

  8. Peñafrancia Festival



    One of the most popular religious festivals in the Philippines is the Peñafrancia Festival in Naga City, Bicol. It is also said to be the biggest Marian festival in Asia.Peñafrancia Festival is regarded as the biggest religious celebration in honor of the Blessed Virgin Mary in Asia. A month-long celebration, it is an annual cultural and religious festival held in honor of Our Lady of Peñafrancia (Spanish: Nuestra Señora de Peñafrancia) in Naga City, Camarines Sur, Philippines every third Sunday of September. Every Friday on the week of the festival is declared a special non-working holiday in the city by virtue of Proclamation No. 654 approved on September 25, 2013.

    Held every third Saturday of September, this week-long event honors the Our Lady of Peñafrancia, the patroness of Bicol (also referred to as “Ina” (by the Bicolanos). It is known for its fluvial procession where the image of the Virgin Mary is transferred from its shrine to the Naga Cathedral via a ,em>pagoda-style barge adorned with bright lights and flowers. Devotees wearing red (which symbolizes the fire of their devotion) paddle the barge along the Naga River while thousands of spectators, holding candles and singing hymns, line up the riverbanks.

    Apart from these, like similar festivals in the country, there is also a much-awaited street parade with participants from different schools wearing colorful outfits and displays.

  2. Kaamulan Festival



    “Kaamulan” is from the Binukid word “amul” which means “to gather”. It is a gathering of Bukidnon tribespeople for a purpose. It can mean a datuship ritual, a wedding ceremony, a thanksgiving festival during harvest time, a peace pact, or all of these put together. An important cultural occasion, the Kaamulan Festival, is held in Malaybalay City, Bukidnon, Philippines.

    The province of Bukidnon was begun on March 10, 1917, which falls on the same day as the Kaamulan Festival. It honors the history, growth, and identity of the province. The seven indigenous tribes of Bukidnon province—the Bukidnon, Higaonon, Talaandig, Manobo, Matigsalug, Tigwahanon, and Umayamnon—come in tandem for the Kaamulan Festival. The festivities provides a stage for highlighting their rich cultural history, customs, and way of life. The festival’s aims are to protect and expand the native tribes’ rich cultural diversity and traditions. It gives the tribes a chance to showcase their arts, music, dances, and rituals to visitors and the larger community.

    To liven up the fiesta celebration, a town official decided to invite some indigenous people to the area and forced them to do a few dance moves in Plaza Rizal. On September 16, 1977, the Regional Development Council of Region 10 proclaimed that the Kaamulan festival was now the regional festival of Northern Mindanao due to the huge popularity of the celebration and its national broadcast.

    Before being changed to the current date in 1996 to coincide with the province's foundation celebration, Kaamulan was held in the first week of September. However, in 2014, in honor of Bukidnon's 100th founding anniversary, it was shifted to the month of August. There was much potential for the festival to be listed among the Intangible Cultural Heritage Lists of UNESCO.

    Quezon, a land whose history is as rich as her heritage. On this special tour, we explore this idyllic province through a crop that has defined the landscape and identity of the region: the coconut.

    The Niyogyugan Festival combination of the words "niyog" and "yugyog"—showcases not only the heritage and culture of Quezon but also the province’s main source of livelihood – coconut. Tagged as the ‘Mother of All Festivals’ in Quezon, the event presents local agricultural products harvested by farmers from the 39 towns and two cities in the province.

    Quezon province’s annual event to celebrate its coconut farmers, resumed after a three-year halt, bringing in an average of 20,000 tourists daily. Quezon is still the top producing province of coconut in the entire Philippines so we celebrate niyog (coconut). Yugyog means a "celebration, to dance, and make it a lively celebration,” Quezon Provincial Tourism Officer Nesler Louies Almagro said in an interview. Coconuts aside, the festival also served as an avenue to reintroduce Quezon to tourists and promote the province’s numerous destinations.

    The Philippines is a country known for its rich cultural heritage and hordes of vibrant festivals that celebrate its traditions and riches. One such event that encapsulates the essence of local Filipino culture is the Niyogyugan Festival which centers on honoring the resilient Coconut Farmers of Quezon Province. Their collated grit and hard work have proven the province as the country’s top producer of Coconut.


    4. Top reasons why Niyogyugan Festival should be on your yearly calendar:

    • A Glimpse into the Grand Niyogyugan Festival

      The province of Quezon, which is situated in the Philippines' Calabarzon region, hosts the Niyogyugan Festival each year. The principal celebrations and events take place in Lucena, the country's capital. The celebration honors the coconut, which the Filipino people like and refer to as the "tree of life" because of its numerous applications in daily life and the hardworking farmers who cultivate it, making the province the world's leading producer of coconuts. Make sure to save August 9–19 on your calendars for this amazing cultural extravaganza, which took place in 2023.


    • The Culminating Grand Parade of Float and Street Dance

      The grand parade, which features colorful floats decorated with materials derived from coconuts and representing each participating municipality in Quezon Province, is the festival's main attraction. With over 30 floats, visitors can expect to be astounded by the sheer volume of participants. The ingenuity and artistry that go into creating these floats will also astound them. Additionally, you'll see vibrant street dancers energizing the atmosphere with their famous Niyogyugan Festival choreography.


    • Delightful Coconut Based Dishes and Treats

      One of the festival’s highlights is the food stalls offering a wide array of coconut-based delicacies. From varied rice cakes to buko pie (coconut pie), you’ll have the opportunity to savor these delectable treats that are unique to the region. Don’t forget to try the fresh buko(young coconut) juice, a refreshing drink that’s perfect for the tropical climate. There is also an array of coconut-based spirits and liquors, and the ever-present in every household – the beloved coconut-based vinegar dip that is approved by the picky Filipino palate. Also, coconut sugar-based sweet treats are almost ubiquitous in every municipal participating booth at the Capitol grounds.


    • Historical and Cultural Experiences

      The Niyogyugan Festival also hosts cultural exhibits and workshops that allow you to immerse yourself in local traditions. You can learn about coconut-based liquor, lambanog production, and other traditional crafts, and even participate in hands-on activities like the Tagayan culture. Young local performers are able to give a series of shows depicting the message of the Quezonian Tagayan culture that is deeply embedded in every aspect of the locals. Get to be involved in the public dance serenaded by local music and narrated by their most respected Tagayan culture custodian, Ms. Tina Decal. It’s a fantastic opportunity to gain a deeper understanding of the cultural significance of the coconut and its participation in the intricate fabric of the locals’ way of life.



    • Souvenirs and Handicrafts at the Municipal Booths

      Don't miss out on exploring the Capitol Grounds' municipal booths and stalls. With nearly 40 participating communities, you'll never run out of take-home snacks and gift ideas. You may buy unique handicrafts and products produced from coconut materials, such as woven bags, caps, and even coconut-based beauty treatments. These gifts offer excellent keepsakes of your Niyogyugan Festival experience. Furthermore, you will be captivated by the imaginative designs of the separate booths displaying various treasures and riches.


    • Celebration of Beauty and Sheer Talent

      Pageantry is a must-have at Philippine celebrations. The Niyogyugan Festival 2023 featured a multi-day pageant event culminating in the spectacular coronation night, which was truly the talk of the town. Each pageant candidate carried their municipality or city's representation on their shoulders, walking the runway with excellent walks and great displays of costume execution portraying their town's bounty. And, as expected, every pageant night drew a roar from the crowd. A province-wide Declamation Contest and Painting Contest provided the opportunity for locals to demonstrate their talents while promoting their individual communities.


    • Tourism Catapult

      Discover the immensity of Quezon Province's tourism potential before or after the festivities. With Lucena City ideally placed at the province's crossroads, you can easily access all of the town's tourist attractions and gastronomic delights, from white sand beaches to towering peaks to walk up. From the northernmost General Nakar town to the southernmost San Francisco town, it would take more than a month to visit every location. Visit Cagbalete Island's broad sand beaches, stroll through Gumaca's ancient ruins, marvel at the highland beauty of Dolores, or rejuvenate your soul at General Nakar. These are only a few of the numerous destinations in Quezon Province.

  4. Bangus Festival



    Bangus Festival Officially named Dagupan City Bangus Festival, is a yearly cultural and food festival in Dagupan City in the province of Pangasinan, Philippines held every April 30. It is a celebration of the city’s production of the bangus fish and many of its related products. Bangus is considered one of the most cultivated fishes that can be found in all regions in the country except for Cordillera Administrative Region, placing the Philippines second place in terms of its production.

    The festivities include an array of sports competitions, fairs, and street parties. And of course, the festival is full of culinary demonstrations, tastings, and contests. See for yourself how a group of chefs demonstrate about 101 different ways to cook bangus. Dagupan is known for its milkfish aquaculture industry and delicious bangus delicacies. Every year, the festival attracts thousands of tourists who come to indulge in Bangusine (bangus cuisine).

    The major highlight of the Dagupan City Bangus Festival is grilling thousands of bangus during an elaborate street party. The festival is considered to be one of the best celebrations in the Philippines. Don't miss that show! A few years ago, about 10,000 fish were cooked on 1000 grills placed along Fernandez Avenue. The event set a world record! The Bangus festival features many street performances that keep visitors entertained between snacking on delicious fish. Numerous dance bands demonstrate their skills and dance with the public so they can experience the local culture. The dancers often wear traditional colourful costumes.



    5. Bangus Festival Schedule of Activities

    Bangus Festival features a busy a calendar of activities such as trade fairs, entertainment, cultural and variety shows, and youth festival. It is usually graced by several artists and musical acts that are known nationally. Competitions are also open for the arts, cuisine, sports.


    Here are some of the highlights.

    • Bangus Rodeo

      Bangus Rodeo is a competition of the best quality bangus. Several categories exist. A category called ‘pretty bangus‘ chooses winners based on the freshness and appearance of the entries of the fish and longest bangus picks the entry with the most length. Skills in handling the fish are also part of the contests: bangus deboner, a race of who can debone the fastest; bangus-eating contest; and fastest bangus classifier, that is one who can separate fishes into specific weight categories.


    • Gilon-Gilon ed Baley Street Dancing

      Gilon-Gilon ed Baley Street Dancing is a street dancing competition involving 31 barangays of Dagupan City. Its name means harvesting or catching of bangus. The competition is characterized by nimble dancers in colorful costumes moving to the beats of the drums and the presence of milkfish as props. Additionally, the gilon-gilon dance is a series of movements that depict the steps in harvesting milkfish, and it was introduced in 1997 as part of the city’s 50th year anniversary of its founding.


    • Kalutan ed Dalan Street Party

      Kalutan ed Dalan translates to barbecue on the street. It is the pinnacle of the festival where visitors can drop by a line of bangus being grilled on spitting coals along the streets of the business district of the city. Moreover it is also a street party. Various musical acts would grace the events to entertain visitors.


    • Pigar Pigar Festival

      Pigar Pigar Festival is a cooking contest of the eponymous specialty dish and street food that has become a favorite and popular. Its recipe is deep-fried thin slices of water buffalo meat and liver served with aromatics (garlic and onion), assortment of vegetables, and sauce. Pigar pigar means to turn over, which is the process of cooking the dish.


    • Pisasalamat ed Ilog

      Pisasalamat ed Ilog translates to river thanksgiving. It is an event in the festival that highlights the river systems in the city, which are source of livelihood for many of its residents particularly the Dawel-Watac River where mangroves abound.

  5. Lechon Festival



    Lechon Festival is a cultural, food, and religious festival held during the feast of St. John the Baptist in the town of Balayan in the province of Batangas,Philippines every June 24. At the center of the celebration is lechon, a whole-pig recipe cooked in an open grill. Lechon Festival is considered the king of festivals in the entire province of Batangas, and it is also called Parada ng Lechon sa Balayan, Batangas. Thus, lechon not only became an expression of thanksgiving as was in the olden times but became as way to venerate the patron.

    Alongside with the names of the mayor and the leading figure in supervising the festival known as hermana mayor. In 1959, an organization called Hermandad San Juan Bautista (Brotherhood of St. John the Baptist) was established and its sole purpose was to manage the activities planned for the whole duration of the festival.

    During the climax of Lechon Festival, water is thrown at passersby, participants, visitors, onlookers, and whoever happens to be within the surrounding area where merry-making is held. Water and its playful, at times riotous, inclusion in the celebration are associated with St. John who baptized Jesus Christ. People drenched by random showers from spalshes thrown at them and carrying roasted pigs skewered by a bamboo pole in a parade have become a distinct aspect of the entire festival.

    The event is filled to the brim with respect to its schedule of activities. It is characterized by a general display of good-natured fun and merrymaking. A few of the activities include competitions in sports, singing, dancing, and other talents; concerts featuring live bands and musical acts; fireworks display; variety and cultural shows; street dancing; and beauty pageant. Balayan is also decorated by streamers and banners, multicolored uniforms representing areas of the town or organizations, and live music.

    Here are some of the highlights of Lechon Festival.

    • Anniversary Ball

      Anniversary Ball occurs the night before the feast day. It is held in the center of the town where the Queen of the festival is crowned.


    • Basaan

      Basaan translates to the activity of sloshing people with water. It is part of the festival and a mark of merry-making, and it is said to remember the baptism of Jesus by St. John. Some footage reveal that firetrucks take part in the revelry, spraying the crowd with jets of water. A few pranksters may douse people with alcoholic beverages.


    • Parada ng Lechon

      Parada ng Lechon translates to parade of roasted pigs and is the highlight of Lechon Festival. It occurs in the morning of the feast day. Lechon that are part of the parade are assembled before the church where they are given blessing by the priest. Then the parade commences. Participants either walk on moving floats or on foot, some of whom are bearing bamboo poles that carry the roasted pig.


    • Religious services

      Religious services such as the nine-day novena prayers and liturgical celebrations are held at the Immaculate Conception Parish Church, the parish in Balayan, Batangas.

     

  6. Dinagyang Festival



    The Dinagyang Festival is a vibrant cultural and religious event held in Iloilo City, Philippines, usually every fourth weekend of January. It is a celebration in honor of the Santo Niño (Infant Jesus) and is known for its colorful street performances, rhythmic drum beats, and elaborate costumes. The festival originated from the traditional Ati-Atihan Festival of the Aeta indigenous people, which was later transformed into the Dinagyang Festival to highlight the Christianization of the natives and their devotion to the Santo Niño.

    During the festival, various tribes or groups known as "tribus" compete in street dancing competitions, showcasing choreographed routines that depict the history, culture, and religious traditions of the region. Participants wear intricate and vibrant costumes adorned with feathers, beads, and other embellishments. In addition to the street performances, there are also religious processions, cultural exhibitions, food fairs, and other activities throughout the festival. The Dinagyang Festival attracts both local and international tourists, making it one of the most popular and anticipated festivals in the Philippines.

    The festival is well-known for its Ati Tribes Competition, featuring various tribu or tribe performers in Ati warrior costumes, presenting traditional choreographed formations, patterns, and rhythmic chanting to the beat of loud drums and improvised percussion instruments, that narrate different iterations of the history of Panay. Another highlight is the Kasadyahan Festival, where different cultural festivals from various places in Western Visayas come together to compete. As the most awarded festival in the country and having been named the best tourism event by the Association of Tourism Officers in the Philippines (ATOP) for many years, the festival is often hailed as the "Queen of All Philippine Festivals.


    7. 5 Events to Look Forward to at the Dinagyang Festival

    • Dinagyang ILOmination and Float Parade of Lights

      The Dinagyang ILOmination is an innovative addition to the Dinagyang Festival. It transforms the traditional dance competition into a parade-type street dance and a procession of illuminated floats. This dynamic and engaging experience combines religious reverence with modern festivities.

    • Kasadyahan sa Kabanwahanan

      The Kasadyahan sa Kabanwahanan—also known as the “Festival of Festivals”—showcases the unique customs and traditions of various municipalities. It forms a rich tapestry of Iloilo's heritage, from the Kaing Festival of the Leon Municipality to the Pantat Festival of the Zarraga Municipality. There are about ten festivals for you to feast your eyes on.

    • Dinagyang Tribes Competition

      The Dinagyang Tribes Competition is a central and thrilling part of the festival, where various tribes compete in lively dance performances. This year, school-based tribes are returning for the first time since 2020. This comeback adds fresh energy and excitement to the event.The dancers wear intricate costumes and body paint to resemble the Ati tribe and perform a choreography that tells a story of devotion and history. Their performance creates a visual spectacle that's both stunning and meaningful.

    • Sadsad sa Calle Real

      The Sadsad sa Calle Real is an interactive street dancing event that lets you join the performers in their celebration. Participating tribes include Tribu Aninipay, Tribu Ibata sa Paghiliugyon, Tribu Molave, Tribu Panaad, and Tribu Parianon. You can dance alongside the tribes and become part of the festival's energy and joy as the celebration continues downtown.

  7. The Masskara Festival



    The Masskara Festival is an annual celebration held in Bacolod City, Philippines. It is known for its vibrant street dances, colorful masks, and lively music. The festival began in the 1980s as a response to economic hardships faced by the city, aiming to uplift the spirits of the people. Participants wear elaborate masks adorned with feathers, beads, and sequins, showcasing creativity and artistry. The festival usually takes place in October, attracting tourists from around the world to witness the unique cultural extravaganza.

    The festival features a street dance competition where people from all walks of life troop to the streets to see masked dancers gyrating to the rhythm of Latin musical beats in a display of mastery, gaiety, coordination and stamina. Major activities include the MassKara Queen beauty pageant, carnivals, drum, bugle corps competitions, food festivals, sports events, musical concerts, agriculture-trade fairs, garden shows, and other special events organized every year.

    Originally, these masks were created just to be sold to tourists and to generate income, and not for a festival that they were yet to conceptualize. The colorful masks created by the local residents were supposedly a signature souvenir of the province and to get the attention of foreign visitors.

    The MassKara has unique and exciting events that even first-time travelers can enjoy. To get the most out of the festivities, here are some tips for first-timers:

    • Wear comfortable shoes and clothes. Lacson Street is the center of the festival and people have to walk a lot to enjoy the event.
    • Bring an umbrella. Weather can be unpredictable and October can either be rainy or sunny. Make sure to check the Bacolod weather report too.
    • Taste the iconic chicken inasal of Bacolod. From food stalls to restaurants, you can spot this chicken dish anywhere you go.
    • Get the best view. Bacolod Public Plaza is the best area to watch the street performances as it is the venue for the dance arena.
    • Be ahead of time. Get on the streets an hour before the start of the festivity. The street dance typically starts at 2 in the afternoon so make sure that you are in a good spot.


    Luckily, the idea was a hit and tourists started buying the masks as they were light and easy to bring around. Visitors can also wear them while touring the Bacolod tourist spots.
